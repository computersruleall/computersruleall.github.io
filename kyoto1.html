100% Exploration

by Pix

The Goal

IM GONNA HIT AS MANY PINS AS POSSIBLE IN 2.5 DAYS

So we need to tackle this efficiently. The biggest time sink here is travel time between locations, so we should minimize travel time by any means necessary.

For Kyoto, this presents an interesting challenge, as the density of locations as well as their placement away from train lines makes walking faster than alternative methods of transportation, with the exception being unless you are traveling cross-city.

The Plan

So this is how we're going to tackle it, starting our day at Kyoto station and branching out in a spider-web manner in order to avoid going back and forth.

Given the limited time-frame, if a pin takes significantly longer to get to in comparison to others in the area or requires a large detour, it is considered for exclusion so that the flow of movement isn't broken.

On Day 1, we will visit the Western side of Kyoto, with the plan looking something like this. Although the start of the day looks rushed, all four locations are extremely close to each other and can be knocked out comfortably without feeling rushed. On the other hand, Kinkakuji and Nijo Castle have been allocated longer time slots in case we want to explore their respective areas.

The reason the day is ended in Osaka's Dotonbori is due to the fact that Kyoto shuts down quite early, with temples closing around 17:00 and local shops a short while after. But we've come all this way and the night is young, so let's go somewhere else that's still open. Dotonbori is about an hour from Kyoto station, so it'll take a bit of time to get there, but since everything else is closed anyways we won't miss out on anything.

In real life, work doesn’t feel good. It doesn’t feel satisfying. It doesn’t feel like you’re getting anywhere. You work a job to get money to live, and if you’re lucky, you can save up to buy some stuff that makes your life a little better. But it never feels like progress. It never feels like building. It just feels like surviving.

Minecraft tricks your brain into thinking you’re being productive. That’s the dark side. That’s why it’s addictive. You feel like you’re accomplishing something. But it’s fake. None of it matters. None of it is real. You didn’t actually build a castle, you just clicked a bunch of times. You didn’t actually plant a farm, you just pushed buttons.

And yet — it feels real. Because the feedback loop is so clean. Because the goals are so clear. Because the work is meaningful, in the context of the world.

I think about that a lot. About how hard it is to find meaningful work in the real world. About how satisfying fake work can feel. About how games like Minecraft give us a glimpse of what it could feel like to build something, if only our world was built differently.

I don’t play Minecraft anymore. But I still dream in cubes.
